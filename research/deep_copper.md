## 1. Copper Intensity Per MW for Data Centers — Source Verification

### The 27 Tonnes/MW Figure — Origin and Chain of Evidence

**Primary Source:** A study of Microsoft's **$500 million data center facility in Chicago** found it consumed **2,177 tonnes of copper** for an ~80 MW applied power load, equating to **27 tonnes of copper per megawatt (MW) of applied power**.

The **original cited source** for this study is:
> *"Why Copper Is Critical for Data Centers"* — **Visual Capitalist**, October 2023  
> Referenced as footnote 4 in BHP's January 2025 analysis.

**BHP's Role:** BHP was the first major mining company to publicize and amplify this figure in a widely-read piece:
> *"Why AI tools and data centres are driving copper demand,"* BHP Insights, January 20, 2025  
> URL: https://www.bhp.com/news/bhp-insights/2025/01/why-ai-tools-and-data-centres-are-driving-copper-demand  
> BHP stated: "A study of Microsoft's US$500 million data centre facility in Chicago found it used 2,177 tonnes of copper, equivalent to **27 tonnes of copper for every megawatt (MW) of applied power**."

**S&P Global's Range:** S&P Global's January 2026 report provides a wider and more nuanced range:
- **30–40 metric tons (mt) Cu per MW** for non-crypto data centers (general range)
- **47 mt Cu/MW** for AI Training data centers (China archetype)
- **21 mt Cu/MW** for crypto/lower-spec facilities
- Example given: A 230 MW AI training data center requires ~10,000 metric tons Cu (~44 mt/MW)
- Source: *"Copper in the Age of AI: Challenges of Electrification"*, S&P Global, January 8, 2026  
  URL: https://www.spglobal.com/en/research-insights/special-reports/copper-in-the-age-of-ai

**The 27–33 Tonnes/MW Range (Broader Industry Usage):**  
The 27–33 tonnes/MW range cited in industry articles (including Tom's Hardware, Yahoo Finance/Reuters December 2025) synthesizes:
- The BHP/Visual Capitalist 27 t/MW (Microsoft Chicago hyperscale baseline)
- Higher-end AI training facility estimates
- Discovery Alert (March 13, 2026) explicitly states hyperscale AI facilities have copper intensity of **~27 tonnes per MW**, representing a 3–4x increase over conventional enterprise data centers (~7–9 t/MW implied)

**BHP Case Study Confirmation:**  
> "BHP's own case studies cite more than **2,000 tonnes for an 80 megawatt-class deployment**" — Tom's Hardware, December 3, 2025  
> URL: https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/ai-data-center-buildout-pushes-copper-toward-shortages-analysts-warn

### Breakdown of Copper Use Within a Data Center (S&P Global / Discovery Alert)

| Infrastructure Layer | Copper Content (kg/MW) | Primary Function |
|---|---|---|
| Power Distribution Systems | 12,000–15,000 | Grid connectivity, transformers |
| Cooling Infrastructure | 8,000–10,000 | Heat exchangers, piping networks |
| Server Hardware & Interconnects | 4,000–6,000 | Motherboards, networking cables |
| Backup Power Systems | 2,000–3,000 | UPS units, battery connections |
| **Total** | **~26,000–34,000 kg/MW** | **= ~26–34 mt/MW** |

Source: https://discoveryalert.com.au/copper-demand-ai-data-centres-2026/

### Is McKinsey a Source for the 27–33 t/MW Figure?
**No.** McKinsey does not appear to be the originating source for the 27–33 t/MW copper intensity figure. McKinsey's copper-related research focuses more on overall demand projections and supply chain economics. The BHP/Visual Capitalist chain (citing the Microsoft Chicago facility) is the primary origin.

### Is IEA a Source for the 27–33 t/MW Figure?
**Indirectly.** The IEA's April 2025 report "Energy and AI" provided first-time estimates of the sector's **mineral requirements** and noted copper as a critical mineral facing increased demand — but did not originate the 27 t/MW figure. The IEA projects data centers will drive **2% of global copper demand by 2030** (per Instagram/IEA data, July 2025).  
Source: https://www.instagram.com/p/DMImQCnqcE2/

### Why Production Is Declining in 2025–2026

Cadia is in a **grade transition phase** as it moves from Panel Cave 1 and Panel Cave 2 (existing producing caves) to the newly-established Panel Cave 2-3 (PC2-3) and the under-development Panel Cave 1-2 (PC1-2). During this transition, the ore being processed has **lower copper and gold grades**, causing a temporary production dip.

Per Newmont's February 2025 guidance:
> "Cadia copper production will also decrease in 2025 as the site transitions to Panel Cave 2-3 and the ongoing work to access higher grades in the future."

Source: https://www.newmont.com/investors/news-release/news-details/2025/Newmont-Reports-Fourth-Quarter-and-Full-Year-2024-Results-Provides-Full-Year-2025-Guidance/default.aspx

## 5. S&P Global Copper Supply Deficit Projection — Verification

### The "10 Million Metric Ton Shortfall by 2040" Claim

**VERIFIED AS ACCURATE.**

Source: *"Copper in the Age of AI: Challenges of Electrification"*, S&P Global, **January 8, 2026**  
Full report URL: https://www.spglobal.com/en/research-insights/special-reports/copper-in-the-age-of-ai  
Reuters coverage: https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/ai-boost-copper-demand-50-by-2040-more-mines-needed-ensure-supply-sp-says-2026-01-08/  
S&P Global Energy news: https://www.spglobal.com/energy/en/news-research/latest-news/metals/010826-copper-supply-gap-to-widen-24-by-2040-as-electrification-accelerates-study

### Exact Numbers from the S&P Global Report

| Year | Demand (MMt Cu) | Primary Mined Supply (MMt Cu) | Recycled Supply (MMt Cu) | Total Supply (MMt Cu) | Shortfall (MMt Cu) |
|------|-----------------|-------------------------------|--------------------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| 2025 | 28 | 23 | 4–5 | ~28 | Balanced |
| 2030 | ~35 | ~27 (peak) | ~7 | ~33–34 | ~1–2 |
| **2040** | **42** | **22** | **10** | **~32** | **~10** |

Key findings per the report:
- **"Without meaningful supply expansion, primary mined supply could decline to 22 MMt by 2040"** — 1 MMt *below* current (2025) production levels
- **"The shortage would be 23.8% shy of projected demand of 42 MMt by 2040"**
- Even recycled copper (growing to 10 MMt, i.e., doubling) cannot close the gap
- **"A 10 MMt supply shortfall represents a systemic risk to global industries"**

Quote from report (co-authored by Daniel Yergin, IHS Markit/S&P vice chairman):
> "Economic demand, grid expansion, renewable generation, AI computation, digital industries, electric vehicles and defense are scaling all at once — and supply is not on track to keep pace."

### Why Supply Is Constrained (S&P Global's Analysis)
1. Declining ore grades at existing mines
2. Permitting timelines averaging **15–17 years** from discovery to production
3. Weak greenfield project pipeline
4. Capital investment constraints: required investment to close the gap estimated at **$200–300 billion** (S&P/referenced in multiple sources)
5. Geopolitical risks in copper-rich Latin America (60% of global supply)

### Is the "10 Mt by 2040" Figure from Other Sources Too?
- **S&P Global is the primary source** for the 10 MMt/2040 figure. The prior S&P Global report (2022, "The Future of Copper") used a net-zero scenario approach.
- Wood Mackenzie (October 2025) estimates a **24% demand surge by 2035 to 42.7 Mtpa**, requiring 8 Mtpa of new mining capacity — consistent directionally.
- IEA has warned of **up to 30% supply shortfall by 2035**, consistent with S&P's findings though slightly different methodology.
- **The 10 MMt figure is specifically and exclusively sourced to S&P Global's January 2026 report.** It should not be attributed to McKinsey, BHP, or IEA.

## 8. Data Gaps and Caveats

1. **JPMorgan primary source not directly verified:** The 475,000 t/yr and 50,000 t/facility figures are attributed to JPMorgan across multiple secondary sources (CarbonCredits, Crux Investor, Forbes), but the original JPMorgan research note is behind a paywall and was not directly fetched. Treat as credibly attributed but not primary-source verified.

2. **McKinsey copper demand report:** McKinsey does not appear to have a recent standalone copper/data center demand report that is publicly accessible. The $5.2 trillion data center investment figure attributed to McKinsey circulates widely but the primary report was not accessible for direct citation.

3. **The 27–33 t/MW range** is derived primarily from the BHP/Visual Capitalist baseline (27 t/MW) plus S&P Global's higher estimates (30–47 t/MW). No single study explicitly states "27–33 t/MW" as a range — it is an industry synthesis.

4. **Cadia expansion copper output total:** The combined post-2030 copper production estimate (~75–115 kt/yr) is a derived estimate from adding the two panel cave peak ranges. Newmont does not publish a single "total Cadia copper 2030" figure in publicly available documents.
